The 10 Best Things at CES 2026

CES 2026 marked a landmark year for inclusive design with mainstream tools that prioritize independence, dignity, and accessibility.

CES 2026 was a landmark year for inclusive design, moving beyond niche gadgets to mainstream tools that prioritize independence, dignity, and accessibility. For anyone who relies on assistive technology, the following ten innovations represent the "coolest" leaps forward from this year's show.

1. .lumen Glasses for the Blind

These glasses act as a technological replacement for a guide dog. Using Pedestrian Autonomous Driving (PAD AI), spatial sensors, and haptic feedback, they guide users through environments in real-time. Unlike a traditional cane, they detect safe walking paths and provide intuitive signals directly to the user, offering scalable mobility for the visually impaired and elderly.

2. HP EliteBoard G1 (The "Blind Dream" PC)

The HP EliteBoard G1 is a full computer built entirely inside a keyboard. It is hailed as the "best blind computer ever made" because it functions independently of a screen. By eliminating the redundant cost and bulk of a monitor, it offers a portable, high-performance workstation for those who rely on screen readers like NVDA.

3. Strutt ev¹: The Smart Mobility Vehicle

Redefining the mobility scooter, the Strutt ev¹ uses LiDAR and 360-degree sensors to navigate crowded areas autonomously. It includes a "Follow Mode," where the vehicle follows its owner during short walks, and a "Waypoints" system that understands natural language commands like "I want some milk" to guide the user directly to the fridge.

4. Nemonic Dot Braille Label Printer

The Nemonic Dot by Mangoslab is a voice-operated Braille printer that allows users to dictate labels via a smartphone app. Priced at $995, it is a game-changer for labeling medication bottles and kitchen items. It can even integrate with pharmacy software to help users identify their prescriptions independently.

5. LG CLOiD: The Household Robot

LG's CLOiD humanoid robot is designed for a "zero-labor home." Using advanced tactile feedback and neural networks, it autonomously handles delicate chores like folding laundry and unloading dishwashers. For those with physical disabilities, CLOiD acts as a practical assistant that can also serve meals and adjust smart home settings.

6. HapWare Wristband for Social Cues

This wearable connects to smart glasses to translate visual social cues into haptic patterns. When the glasses' AI detects a facial expression—like a smile or a frown—the wristband vibrates in a specific pattern, allowing the user to "feel" non-verbal cues in real-time. This provides an essential sensory layer for people who struggle to read expressions.

7. Naqi Neural Earbuds

For users who cannot use their hands or voice to control devices, the Naqi Neural Earbuds provide an invisible user interface. By sensing subtle, nearly invisible facial gestures, these earbuds allow users to command their digital environment without external input, acting as a non-invasive alternative to brain implants.

8. Seattle Ultrasonics C200 Knife

Cooking can be a barrier for those with arthritis or limited dexterity. The C200 chef knife uses vibrating PZO ceramic crystals to make a blade behave as if it is sharper than it is, requiring 50% less force to cut through food. This application of material science makes slicing potatoes or vegetables effortless and safer for users with low hand strength.

9. Sumbu Exo-S3: Consumer Exoskeleton

The Exo-S3 is one of the first commercially available exoskeletons priced for the general public at $1,199. It uses Recognition AI to sense human intent, boosting movement efficiency by 20%. It is designed for daily mobility support and active injury prevention, making walking and endurance tasks easier for the average user.

10. 360 Direct Access Sign Language Chatbot

This platform introduces the first AI-powered sign language chatbot for real-world customer service. Powered by DeepSign AI, it recognizes American Sign Language (ASL) in real-time via video call, allowing Deaf users to interact with customer service agents through their native language without waiting for a human interpreter.

Analogy for the 2026 CES shift:
In the past, assistive tech was like a specialized tool kept in a separate drawer; this year, it has become the "multi-tool" in everyone's pocket—highly advanced, mainstream, and essential for navigating the world with ease.
